current-production XLM
late 80s Merlin titanium

At 3.1lbs the Merlin XLM is the lightest frame I own. This double-butted 3/2.5 titanium hardtail has already taken a mean lickin' but still the plan is to keep it a long time.

It rides especially well - stable but fast - but it springs out of shape a little when slamming into bumps at speed. Also it would be flexy for a big rider. And a Yeti FRO is a tough act to follow!

My Yeti was raced many seasons up through 2001. It was several lbs heavier than other guys' bikes but it was when the chainstay cracked that I thought about buying a new bike. I considered getting a scandium frame made by my local framebuilding buddy Andy Gilmour, but I realized the way I am I'll want to keep it forever and so titanium was a better choice.

This Merlin appealed to me as a long-perfected design, stretching back over a decade and racking up wins in the big races. The history matters to me, and maybe to other fools as well. The owners of the older classic Merlins keep theirs forever, still ridden hard.

They are definitely expensive so I waited for the right used one to come along. I built it up to be pretty light for racing but trusty enough to take way out in the sticks. Since I don't like upgrading (much less maintaining) my bikes, I am going to resist getting the next generation of XTR bits nor will I be getting a fully-sussed freeridin'-dude bike for another 3-5 years.

This one's original owner Steve got tired of the attention the bike received, so he put Harley-Davidson decals on it. According to Steve this frame came with "Designed by Rob Vandermark" decals. A real guru with titanium, Rob Vandermark was the designer at Merlin up until around this time in '97 when he moved on to be president of Seven Cycles. There's kind of a New England family tree of mtn bikes that starts with Fat Cycles and carries through in Merlin and Independant Fabrications.
